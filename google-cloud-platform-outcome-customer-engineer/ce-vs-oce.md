
Google Cloud has explicitly differentiated these two roles to address a common challenge in the cloud industry: ensuring that the massive cloud commitments customers sign on paper actually translate into successfully running workloads in production.

Here is the up-to-date breakdown of the scopes, responsibilities, and key differences between a **Customer Engineer (CE)** and an **Outcome Customer Engineer (OCE)**.

## 1. Customer Engineer (CE) — _The Pre-Sales Architect_

The traditional Customer Engineer role is heavily focused on the **pre-sales phase** of the customer lifecycle. They act as the ultimate technical bridge between a customer's business challenges and Google Cloud's portfolio.

- **The Core Mission:** Win the technical selection. The CE's job is to convince prospective customers that Google Cloud is the absolute best platform for their needs.
    
- **Key Responsibilities:**
    
    - Partnering with Account Executives (sales reps) to pitch Google Cloud solutions to CIOs, CTOs, and engineering leads.
        
    - Designing high-level, complex cloud architectures and running Proof of Concepts (PoCs) to prove the technology works.
        
    - Handling technical objections and highlighting Google’s differentiators against competitors like AWS and Microsoft Azure.
        
- **Primary Metrics:** Signed contracts, technical wins, and total committed contract value (TCV).
    

> **In short:** They paint the vision, architect the initial solution, and help close the deal.

## 2. Outcome Customer Engineer (OCE) — _The Post-Sales Delivery Champion_

The Outcome Customer Engineer is a newer, highly specialized role focused on the **post-sales deployment phase**. Once a customer signs the contract, the OCE steps in to turn those promises into reality.

- **The Core Mission:** Accelerate "time-to-value" and drive actual cloud consumption. Cloud providers only make money when customers _use_ the resources they bought; the OCE ensures that happens smoothly.
    
- **Key Responsibilities:**
    
    - Developing and managing structured deployment plans to transition scoped workloads into live production environments.
        
    - Getting into the technical trenches—applying hands-on skills like coding, debugging, and systems design to clear technical roadblocks.
        
    - Coordinating with third-party partners and the customer's internal teams to ensure readiness (e.g., proper training, capacity planning).
        
    - Identifying expansion opportunities and ensuring high satisfaction to secure future contract renewals.
        
- **Primary Metrics:** Consumption velocity (how fast the customer spins up workloads), production milestones achieved, and realized business value.
    

> **In short:** They take what was sold, unblock technical hurdles, and ensure the customer successfully deploys it.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

|**Feature**|**Customer Engineer (CE)**|**Outcome Customer Engineer (OCE)**|
|---|---|---|
|**Primary Phase**|Pre-Sales (Before the contract is signed)|Post-Sales (After the contract is signed)|
|**Core Objective**|Secure the technical win and close the deal|Move customer from "agreement" to "production"|
|**Day-to-Day Activity**|High-level architecture, pitches, and PoCs|Hand-on debugging, deployment planning, project leadership|
|**Internal Partners**|Account Executives, Product Managers|Customer Success, Professional Services, Partners|
|**Compensation focus**|Tied heavily to regional/pod sales quotas|Tied to workload consumption and project delivery|

## Why Google Split the Roles

This structural split is a win-win. By separating the roles, Google ensures that **CEs** can stay hyper-focused on hunting new business and designing initial solutions, while **OCEs** provide the dedicated, deep technical project management required to prevent projects from stalling post-sale. One maps the dream; the other builds the reality.
